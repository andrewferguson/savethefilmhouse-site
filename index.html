Edinburgh International Film Festival

Established in 1947, the Edinburgh International Film Festival (EIFF) is the oldest continually running film festival in the world. Showing a range of feature-films, shorts, documentaries and animation, the festival acts as a hub for Scottish, UK and International film.

The Festival has been a champion of emerging British talent throughout its history, presenting world premieres of formative films by Bill Forsyth, Danny Boyle and Stephen Frears, among many others. Over the years, EIFF has welcomed a huge number of guests, including John Huston, Gene Kelly, Jacques Tati, Jennifer Lawrence, Tilda Swinton, Ewan McGregor, Robert Carlyle, David Cronenberg, Cate Blanchett, Clint Eastwood and Sir Sean Connery. The Festival has hosted UK premieres of Blade Runner, Alien, Back to the Future, Taxi Driver, Annie Hall, Withnail & I, The Usual Suspects, Amelie, The Hurt Locker and many, many more.

Filmhouse Edinburgh

Filmhouse is a world-class, three-screen independent cinema showing a wide range of films from all over the world, from quality crowd-pleasers and family films to esoteric arthouse works, via restored classics, retrospectives and themed seasons and festivals.

Filmhouse's programme includes around 700 films and events every year, both digitally and from film, be it 35mm, 16mm or even 70mm. Q&As and panel discussions are common, as is a range of workshops, courses and events for all ages. All three cinemas are fully accessible, and regularly screen films with audio description and hard-of-hearing subtitles (2012 Go Shop Award from the RNIB).

Belmont Filmhouse

Originally constructed as a town hall, Belmont's history as a film venue stretches back to 1898. In 1910 it was converted to a permanent cinema, and operated until the 1950s. Since 2014 it was operated by the Centre for the Moving Image as the only independent cinema in Aberdeen.

As with the Filmhouse Edinburgh, the Belmont's programme is rich and diverse, from recent independent releases, to past hits, to foreign-language films. Many showings are available in either captioned, subtitled or audio-described formats, ensuring that cinema is accessible to all.
